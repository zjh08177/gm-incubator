## Q1 — "Can I build a system around f7 attacks for White at 1800+?"

**Answer A**
> f7 is the classic target — early on only the king defends it. The coherent White system is 1.e4 e5 with Bc4 + Ng5 ideas: the Italian (Giuoco/Two Knights) into the Fried Liver and Lolli, with Scotch Gambit and Bishop's Opening move orders transposing in; add the Smith-Morra vs the Sicilian for a similar Bc4-into-f7 setup. At 1800+ opponents defend f7 more accurately, so don't force sacrifices — treat f7 pressure as a positional lever (pin the f7-knight, pile up, provoke a weakness) and only sac when you've calculated it concretely. Build a small file of the main Fried Liver lines and the critical defenses (…d5, …Na5). Against non-1…e5 replies (Caro, French, Sicilian proper) you'll need separate systems — f7 pressure is an anti-1…e5 tool, not a universal plan.

**Answer B**
> Verdict: you already run an f7 system — the data says lean harder on two lines, don't build something new. Your f7-aimed White lines score: Scotch Gambit 0.59 (107g), Smith-Morra 0.65 (66g), and the Bishop's/Italian Ng5 complex clears 55%; your Blackmar-Diemer complex (0.56–0.64) is the same attacking DNA a rank up. The catch is coverage: an f7 system only exists against 1…e5 (and the Sicilian via Smith-Morra). Against the Caro-Kann, French, Modern and Pirc there's no f7 target — and those are a large share of what you actually face, so "a system around f7" can never be your whole White game. Rep: standardize the Scotch Gambit + Bishop's-Opening Ng5 as your 1…e5 f7 backbone (both already score); keep your existing anti-Caro/French lines for everything else. Consolidate, don't rebuild.

## Q2 — "Should I switch from 1.e4 to 1.d4 because BDG scores well?"

**Answer A**
> Verdict: no — and you don't need to, because you already play the BDG under a d4 move order without giving up e4. Your BDG lines score across a big sample — Ryder Gambit 0.56 (407g), main 0.56 (381g), Zeller 0.64 (100g) — but they only arise after 1.d4 d5 2.e4 dxe4. Commit to 1.d4 as a full system and you walk into the King's Indian, Grünfeld, Slav and QGD, where you have almost no games and no gambit; worse, 1.d4 Nf6 or 2…e6/…c6 dodges the BDG entirely and dumps you back into the French/Caro structures you already play. Meanwhile your real e4 leak is the French-as-White middlegame (Normal 0.46 over 439, Advance 0.40) — fixing that gains more than a switch ever would. Rep: keep 1.e4; wire the BDG in only as 1.d4 d5 2.e4; spend the study time on French pawn-break plans, not a move-order swap.

**Answer B**
> Probably not. The BDG (1.d4 d5 2.e4 dxe4 3.Nc3) is a specific gambit, not a reason to abandon 1.e4 — and it only appears if Black plays 1…d5. Against 1.d4 Nf6, or 1…d5 2.e4 e6/c6, you're in different territory (Indian defenses, Slav, French/Caro transpositions), most of which are slower, more strategic games than the sharp attacking chess you seem to like. Switching your entire first move is a big investment for one gambit line. If you want the BDG, you can reach it via 1.d4 d5 2.e4 while keeping 1.e4 as your main move. Only commit to 1.d4 if you actually want to learn mainline Queen's-pawn structures; otherwise deepen what already works and patch your worst 1.e4 lines instead.

## Q3 — "Why do I keep losing winning attacks?"

**Answer A**
> Usually it's one of three things: you overextend and the attack was never fully sound; you calculate your own ideas but not the opponent's defensive resources and counter-shots; or you sacrifice on general grounds without concrete calculation. At around your level the second is most common — you see your threats, not his. Fixes: before committing to an attacking move, force yourself to find the opponent's best defense and any counterattack (checks, captures, threats); don't sacrifice unless you can calculate a concrete follow-up; keep your own pieces defended so a failed attack doesn't just hang material. Do daily tactics puzzles that emphasize defense and calculation, and review your lost "winning" games to find the exact move it flipped. Patience at the critical moment beats speed.

**Answer B**
> Verdict: your attacks don't fail for lack of ideas — they fail because you hang material walking into his reply. Keep the fire; fix the defense. You drop a piece outright (`dropped_material`) on 71,517 of your moves — 48,813 in the middlegame — and it barely eases with time in hand (68,456 had-time), so it's judgment, not the clock. It's now your single costliest error by win% lost, and 56% are punished by an immediate capture on the very next move. Your own missed shots are rare by comparison (`missed_tactic` 9,234 — and that only counts skipped winning captures). That's *consistent with* attacks dying to his counter-shot; to prove it's your attacks specifically, run `gm find-positions --error-type dropped_material --phase middlegame`. Rep: before every forcing move (a sac, a pawn-grab, a tension-releasing capture) scan his checks/captures/threats and your own loose pieces — 15 "find HIS threat" reps a day. Checkpoint: your had-time `dropped_material` rate over the next 100 games.
